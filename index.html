How I think

Three things I believe
that most teams don't act on.

I

Platform thinking is how AI teams move from building agents to building governable agents.

Teams were focused on agents, skills, plugins — writing prompts. No one was thinking about governance, cost monitoring, prompt optimization, testing. I built guardrails, observability, and evaluation frameworks like DeepEvals into the platform itself. When these become built-in options instead of add-ons, teams adopt them naturally.

II

Multi-agent AI is being sold as magic. The hard part isn't the agents — it's the substrate.

You can build agents in weeks. Building the substrate that lets them run safely in production takes months — audit trails, cost attribution, deterministic testing, graceful failure modes. This isn't anti-agent. It's pro-substrate-first.

III

FinOps isn't cost-cutting. Done right, it's an architectural forcing function.

Cost reduction was the headline. The real outcome: an architecture that could actually be maintained and evolved by the team. When FinOps forces you to consolidate and standardize, governance becomes possible. If your FinOps work isn't also simplifying architecture, you're missing the point — and probably optimizing the wrong layer.

★ Hackathon → Production Multi-Agent AI Security Operations

Multi-agent vulnerability triage — built substrate-first.

80%

60%

The problem
High-volume security triage — reading findings, assessing exploitability, deciding escalation. Mechanical work burning out skilled analysts.
The decision
Build orchestration substrate before agents: agent contracts, routing with human-in-loop for ambiguous findings, cost attribution, eval pipelines. Agents on AWS Bedrock, AgentCore, and Lambda — extended with Claude-based skills and plugins for natural language interaction with the triage workflow — tuneable without rewriting routing.
The tradeoff
Substrate-first delays visible results. I made the case: teams skipping this step rebuild later at 3× cost, under pressure. That framing bought the time.
What I'd do differently
Involve analysts in agent design from day one. Their mental model was more nuanced than our encoding — iterating with them up front beats re-tuning later.

In-house container platform — eliminating vendor dependency, saving millions.

The problem
Vendor cluster management was expensive, rigid, and couldn't serve the self-service and governance needs of a company-wide Internal Developer Platform. We needed full lifecycle control — not a tool that fit 70% of the need and blocked the rest.
The decision
The Internal Developer Platform was the bigger initiative I designed and led — a company-wide self-service infrastructure platform. Container management was its first major delivery: in-house EKS lifecycle management with automated provisioning, GitOps-based config, and Karpenter for dynamic compute. Kubernetes consolidation (reducing cluster estate by ~68%) proved the platform's value at exactly the right moment.
The case: vendor lock-in compounds silently. Build the abstraction now or pay in flexibility later.
The tradeoff
In-house means owning every EKS upgrade, every Karpenter change, every baseline update. For platform primitives, ownership always beats convenience.
What I'd do differently
Run a cost model with multi-year projections. "Break-even by Q2, ROI by Q4" is more credible than "we'll save money."

Point of view

On the AI platform
most teams will build wrong.

The interesting work in enterprise AI right now isn't model selection. It's governance, evaluation, and monitoring around LLM consumption. That's where behavior becomes predictable, costs become attributable, and AI earns trust at scale.

The opportunity isn't hosting models. It's building the frameworks that make LLM consumption safe, measurable, and improvable.

What's missing: evaluation pipelines that catch regressions before users do. Cost attribution that shows which prompts burn money. Guardrails that prevent PII leakage. Monitoring that surfaces when responses degrade. Teams skipping this layer are solving yesterday's problem. I've built parts of this, and the hardest part is never the tooling.

The hardest part isn't technical. It's getting security, compliance, and eng to agree on "production-ready" before the first API call ships.

The next challenges are systems problems: consistency across multi-agent workflows, graceful degradation in LLM chains, cost controls that don't break UX. Solving them takes depth in both systems engineering and production AI. That's the substrate I build.
